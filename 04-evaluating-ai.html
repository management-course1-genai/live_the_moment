Step 1 / 9 The only question that matters

The simplest question separates the two: how do you know the system is actually working?

One happy demo is a feeling. A measurement is an answer.

Behind one good demo sits a stack of outputs you never tested.

"It worked when I tried it" tells you about one try — not about the system.

Evaluation turns AI from a magic trick into a tool you can rely on.

It feels like extra work on top of the build. It is not extra work — it is the work.

A model you haven't evaluated is a model you cannot defend.

Not to a regulator, a client, a colleague — or to yourself when it breaks at 11pm on a Friday.

Hold every output up to three lenses in turn.

Faithfulness, relevance, format compliance — three plain questions, applied to each result.

Faithfulness: does the output stay true to the input — facts preserved, constraints honoured?

The most important lens whenever the model is summarising, extracting, or transforming source material.

Relevance: does the output address what was actually asked?

It is depressingly common to get a fluent, accurate answer — to a question you never asked.

Format compliance: did it follow the structure you asked for — length, tone, sections, fields, citations?

An output that ignores the format is unusable downstream, even when the content is good.

LLM-as-judge DEFINITION — one model scores another's output against a rubric you design.

Invaluable for scaling routine quality checks — but not a substitute for human judgement on the hardest cases. This week: build a rubric, score ten outputs, find one honest failure.
