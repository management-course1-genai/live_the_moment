1The rhythm of reasoning

Ask it to show its working

Remember being told to "show your working" in school? It turns out language models are the same: ask them to reason step by step and they get multi-step problems right far more often. We'll watch it on a genuinely high-stakes task — turning a dense hospital discharge note into something a tired, anxious patient can actually follow, without losing a single instruction. Why healthcare? When a manufacturing assistant invents a number, the cost is rework and an awkward conversation. When a healthcare assistant softens a dosage, the cost can be a readmission — or worse. It's the sharpest test of whether your design embeds real oversight by default.

Definition
Chain-of-thought prompting

A prompt instruction that asks the model to reason through a problem step by step before giving its final answer. The intermediate reasoning is often visible in the response — which is itself useful, because it lets you spot where the model went wrong if the final answer is off. The better models now do this even when you don't ask.

Oversight you can actually perform

Visible reasoning turns "trust the black box" into "check the working" — the difference between oversight you claim on paper and oversight you can really do. Because the steps were on the page, you could catch the slip before it ever reached the patient.

3Knowing when not to answer

Know when to stop

A good assistant — like a good colleague — knows when to say "that's not mine to answer; let me point you to someone who can." It declines when a request is out of scope, unsafe, or when it simply doesn't have the information. Refuse too much and it's useless. Refuse too little and it's dangerous. Definition
Refusal

The behaviour of an assistant when it correctly declines to answer — because the question is out of scope, the action is unsafe, or the necessary information is missing. A well-designed assistant refuses gracefully and helpfully: it explains, points you somewhere useful, and never invents what it doesn't know. A poor one refuses too much (annoying) or too little (dangerous).

The whole picture

Two habits, one goal. Reasoning out loud lets a human check the working before trusting the answer. Graceful refusal draws a safe boundary by default. Put together, they're how you make dense, scary instructions easier to follow without losing anything that matters — which is the real professional skill.

Three habits of a careful assistant

No code, no jargon — just three ideas you can feel. Here's the whole thing in three lines:

Show the working
Step-by-step reasoning keeps the specifics a quick answer drops.
Check the working
Visible steps let a human catch the slip before it does harm.
Know when to stop
Decline what's unsafe, out of scope, or unknown — gracefully, never by inventing.
