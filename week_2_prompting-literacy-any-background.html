02 The four techniques

Same task, four ways of asking

Each tab keeps your case study fixed and changes only how you ask. Press the buttons, read what comes back, and feel the difference for yourself.

Zero-shot

You just describe what you want — no examples — and let the model produce it. What most people do most of the time. Great for common asks, shaky for anything specialised.

The catch: the answer sounds confident and fluent — but the structure is loose and you only discover a weak prompt by reading a plausible, wrong result. Fine for a rough draft; not yet dependable.

One-shot

You give one example of input → the output you want, then ask for the same on new input. A huge reliability jump whenever the shape of the answer matters.

What changed: one example locked the format. The output now lands in a consistent, reusable shape — ready to drop into a doc, sheet, or system.

Few-shot

You give a few examples before asking. For sorting, tagging and extracting, the examples teach the model the boundaries between your categories. Flip the switch and watch reliability jump.

Correctly sorted—

With no examples the model guesses your categories and mislabels the tricky ones. A handful of examples teach it where each category begins and ends — the workhorse move for any sorting or tagging job.

Role

You tell the model who it is. The role sets tone, vocabulary and caution. It doesn't change the facts — it changes the voice. Pick a role and run it.

03 The capstone

Write the assistant's standing instructions

The system prompt is the first instruction a model gets, before any message — it sets the role, the rules, the format and the refusals. (It's exactly what you type into a custom GPT or Project setup screen.) Toggle the parts, send a test message, and watch the behaviour change.
