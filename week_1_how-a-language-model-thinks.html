1The one idea underneath everything

It just guesses the next word

You already do this. When a friend texts "Are we still on for…", your brain fills in the rest before you read it. That's exactly what a language model does — at enormous scale. Pick a sentence below, guess the next word yourself, then see what the model expects. You'll often agree with it.

In one line
A prediction engine

Given the text so far, the model predicts what is most likely to come next — then again, and again. To do that well, it has to absorb grammar, context, tone and subject matter. Whether that's "real" understanding is a question for philosophers; the practical point is simpler: it's sophisticated enough to be useful, and unreliable enough to need your judgement.

2How the machine reads

It reads in chunks, not letters

Before the model reads your message, it chops the text into small pieces called tokens — a bit like reading in LEGO bricks instead of single letters. A token is often a whole word, sometimes just a fragment like "ing". Type anything — your name, a message, a long word — and watch it break apart.

Definition
Token

A small chunk of text — often a whole word, but sometimes a fragment such as "ing" or "ation". When you send a prompt, your words are first broken into tokens. The model thinks in tokens, not in words, which is why it occasionally makes peculiar mistakes with spelling or counting letters. Rough rule of thumb: 100 tokens ≈ 75 words.

3How much it can hold in mind

Its memory is a desk, not a filing cabinet

The model can only "see" a certain amount of text at once — its context window. Picture a colleague with a small desk: only so many pages fit, and when new ones arrive, the oldest slide off the edge. Shrink the desk below and watch the start of the conversation disappear — then ask about it.

Definition
Context window

The amount of text — measured in tokens — the model can hold in mind at once. Modern systems range from roughly 8,000 tokens (about 6,000 words) to over a million. When you upload a long document, the system fits as much as it can; anything that falls outside is, for that conversation, forgotten. It hasn't truly forgotten — the assistant simply stopped including it.

4The creativity dial

Temperature: steady, or surprising?

One hidden setting decides how adventurous the model is. Think of a barista: turn it down and you get the same reliable latte every time; turn it up and they start inventing new drinks — exciting, occasionally odd. Same request, different dial. Generate a few names at each setting and feel the difference.

Definition
Temperature

A setting that controls how predictable the output is. Low temperature → consistent, conservative answers, good for facts, summaries and structured output. High temperature → more variation and creativity, good for brainstorming, story-writing and ideation. In most consumer chat apps it's set for you and hidden; in business tools you can usually adjust it.

The whole picture

That's the machine: it guesses the next token, it reads in chunks, it holds a limited window in mind, and a dial tunes how adventurous it is. Everything astonishing you've seen AI do grows from those four simple things — which is exactly why it's powerful enough to lean on, and quirky enough to keep checking.
